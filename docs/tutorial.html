01 · Theory

The Prisoner's Dilemma

Two strangers. One choice each. No communication. The oldest tension in game theory mirrors some of the deepest puzzles in biology.

The Prisoner's Dilemma (PD) was formalised by Merrill Flood and Melvin Dresher at RAND Corporation in 1950 and given its narrative by Albert Tucker. Two players independently choose to either Cooperate (C) or Defect (D). Critically, defecting is always individually rational, yet mutual cooperation yields a better collective outcome.

This tension, individual rationality vs. collective optimality, is at the heart of phenomena as diverse as:

  • How bacteria share public goods in biofilms
  • Why cleaner fish don't simply eat their clients
  • Arms races between nations
  • Price wars between competitors
  • Evolution of altruism and social norms
Why it matters: If defection is always individually better, how did cooperation evolve at all? Spatial structure, memory, reputation, and punishment are nature's answers, and are all modelled in this simulation.

The Original Story

Two suspects are arrested. They cannot communicate. The prosecutor offers each: if you betray the other while they stay silent, you go free and they get 3 years (T = Temptation). If you both stay silent, you each get 1 year (R = Reward for mutual cooperation). If both betray, you each get 2 years (P = Punishment for mutual defection). If you stay silent and they betray, you get 3 years (S = Sucker's payoff).

Regardless of what the other person does, betraying (defecting) gives you a shorter sentence. Yet both betraying gives a worse outcome than both staying silent. This is the dilemma.

02 · Mathematics

Payoff Matrix & Formal Conditions

The game has four outcomes. Their relative magnitudes define whether the game is a true Prisoner's Dilemma, and shape all of the evolutionary dynamics.

Opponent: Cooperate Opponent: Defect
You: Cooperate R, R
Reward (3, 3)		 S, T
Sucker / Temptation (0, 5)
You: Defect T, S
Temptation / Sucker (5, 0)		 P, P
Punishment (1, 1)

The Two Fundamental Conditions

For a game to be a genuine Prisoner's Dilemma, the following strict ordering must hold:

Condition 1, Temptation ordering $$T > R > P > S$$

This says: the best individual outcome is exploiting a cooperator (T), followed by mutual cooperation (R), then mutual defection (P), and worst is being exploited (S).

Condition 2, No benefit from alternating exploitation $$2R > T + S$$

Without this constraint, two players could do better by taking turns exploiting each other, converting the game into a coordination game rather than a dilemma. With it, sustained mutual cooperation is the global optimum.

The simulation validates both conditions live. In the control panel, you'll see a ✓ green label when your payoff values satisfy the PD conditions, or a ⚠ red warning otherwise. Setting values that violate the conditions produces qualitatively different (non-PD) dynamics.

Dominant Strategy Analysis

In a single, one-shot game, defection is a strictly dominant strategy for rational agents. The proof is a simple comparison:

Proof of dominance $$\text{If opponent cooperates: } T > R \implies \text{Defect is better}$$ $$\text{If opponent defects: } P > S \implies \text{Defect is still better}$$ $$\therefore \text{ Defecting dominates regardless of opponent's choice}$$

The unique Nash Equilibrium of the one-shot PD is therefore (Defect, Defect), a Pareto-inefficient outcome, both players could be better off if they could credibly commit to cooperating.

Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma

When the game is played repeatedly (Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma, IPD), the analysis changes dramatically. With an infinite (or indefinitely long) horizon, the Folk Theorem guarantees that cooperation can be sustained as a Nash Equilibrium:

Folk Theorem, discount factor condition for cooperation $$\delta \geq \frac{T - R}{T - P}$$

Where $\delta \in [0,1)$ is the discount factor (probability the game continues, or how much future payoffs are valued). With the default values ($T=5, R=3, P=1$), cooperation is sustainable when $\delta \geq \frac{5-3}{5-1} = \frac{1}{2}$. Players who care enough about the future can profitably cooperate.

Average Payoff Notation

For a strategy $s_i$ playing against strategy $s_j$ repeatedly, the expected payoff per round converges to a steady-state that depends on the strategies' interaction pattern. For TFT vs TFT (both cooperating every round):

Expected payoff, mutual cooperators $$\bar{\pi}(\text{TFT} | \text{TFT}) = R = 3$$
Expected payoff, AllD exploiting AllC $$\bar{\pi}(\text{AllD} | \text{AllC}) = T = 5, \quad \bar{\pi}(\text{AllC} | \text{AllD}) = S = 0$$
03 · Agents

The Six Strategies

Each cell on the grid houses an agent with a fixed decision rule. These rules range from naïve to vindictive, and interact in surprising ways.

Always Cooperate

Unconditional Dove

Always plays C regardless of history. Maximises social welfare when paired with another cooperator (R + R = 6 per round), but is fatally exploited by defectors (scoring only S = 0). Thrives only in a population dominated by cooperators.

Always Defect

Unconditional Hawk

Always plays D. Scores T against cooperators and P against defectors. Invades cooperator populations but collapses when it dominates, as D-D interactions yield only P. The "ecological" endpoint of defection is mutual impoverishment.

Tit-for-Tat

Kind Reciprocator

Cooperates on the first move, then mirrors the opponent's last move. Axelrod's famous tournaments showed TFT won even though it never "beats" any opponent, its success comes from doing well with other cooperators. Formally: $a_t = a_{t-1}^{\text{opp}}$.

Grim Trigger

The Unforgiving Punisher

Cooperates until the opponent defects once, then defects forever. Theoretically optimal for sustaining cooperation (the harshest credible punishment), but catastrophic under noise or mutation. Memory: a single boolean flag per opponent.

Pavlov

Win-Stay, Lose-Shift

If opponent cooperated last round, keep your move. If opponent defected last round, switch your move. Formally: $ a_t = \begin{cases} a_{t-1} & \text{if } a_{t-1}^{\text{opp}} = C \\ \neg a_{t-1} & \text{if } a_{t-1}^{\text{opp}} = D \end{cases} $ , forgiving unlike Grim Trigger, corrects mistakes.

Random

Stochastic Baseline

Plays C or D with equal probability $p = 0.5$ independently each round. Acts as a noise baseline. Expected payoff against itself: $\frac{1}{4}(R + T + S + P) = \frac{R+T+S+P}{4}$ which equals $\frac{9}{4} = 2.25$ under default values.

Memory Structure in Code

Each agent maintains a HashMap<usize, InteractionHistory> indexed by opponent ID. The history record stores: 

pub struct InteractionHistory {
    pub opponent_last_move:    Option<Move>,
    pub my_last_move:          Option<Move>,
    pub last_outcome:          f32,
    pub round_number:          usize,
    pub opponent_defected_ever: bool, // for Grim Trigger
}
Rust

Using a HashMap (sparse storage) is critical: on a 100×100 grid, each agent interacts with at most 8 neighbours per generation. Storing the full N×N interaction matrix would require 10,000 × 10,000 = 100M entries, 400 MB for 32-bit floats. The sparse map typically holds 4–16 entries per agent.

Payoff Lookup

The payoff function is a pure function, no mutable state, safe to parallelise: 

fn calculate_payoff(&self, move1: Move, move2: Move) -> (f32, f32) {
    match (move1, move2) {
        (Move::Cooperate, Move::Cooperate) => (self.config.payoff_r, self.config.payoff_r),
        (Move::Defect,    Move::Defect)    => (self.config.payoff_p, self.config.payoff_p),
        (Move::Cooperate, Move::Defect)    => (self.config.payoff_s, self.config.payoff_t),
        (Move::Defect,    Move::Cooperate) => (self.config.payoff_t, self.config.payoff_s),
    }
}

04 · Spatial Model

Space Changes Everything

The mean-field PD predicts defectors win. Spatial structure breaks this prediction, cooperators can survive by clustering together, just like species do in nature.

The Toroidal Grid

Agents occupy cells on a 2D lattice of size $W \times H$. The grid wraps toroidally: the left edge connects to the right, and the top connects to the bottom. This eliminates boundary effects and makes the environment spatially uniform.

Cell $(x, y)$ wraps to cell $(x \bmod W,\ y \bmod H)$. Internally, the 2D grid is stored as a flat 1D vector: index $i = y \cdot W + x$.

Neighbour coordinates (Moore neighbourhood, toroidal) $$\mathcal{N}(x,y) = \{\ ((x+dx) \bmod W,\ (y+dy) \bmod H)\ :\ (dx,dy) \in [-1,1]^2 \setminus \{(0,0)\}\ \}$$

Every cell has exactly $|\mathcal{N}| = 8$ neighbours, the Moore neighbourhood.

The Neighbourhood Parameter $N$

Rather than always interacting with fixed neighbours, the simulation exposes a continuous locality parameter $N \in [0, 1]$:

Opponent selection probability $$P(\text{play neighbour}) = N, \quad P(\text{play random}) = 1 - N$$
  • $N = 1.0$, Pure local: you only interact with your 8 Moore neighbours
  • $N = 0.0$, Pure random: each game is against a uniformly random agent
  • $N = 0.5$, Mixed: half your games are local, half global
Key finding: At $N=1$ cooperators form tight spatial clusters that are resistant to invasion by defectors. At $N=0$ the system approximates the mean-field, and defectors generally win. The phase transition between these regimes is one of the most interesting things to observe in the simulation.

Interaction Symmetry

To avoid counting interactions twice (agent $i$ challenging $j$ and $j$ challenging $i$ separately), games are generated using half-neighbours: only the 4 neighbours reachable by the offsets $(+1, 0),\ (+1, +1),\ (0, +1),\ (-1, +1)$. This covering set tiles the plane and ensures each spatial pair plays exactly once per generation. 

let offsets = [(1, 0), (1, 1), (0, 1), (-1, 1)];
// Each pair (i, j) with i < j appears exactly once
Rust

Random pairings ($N < 1$) can generate any agent as the opponent, so these are generated directionally (agent $i$ selects the random opponent). This means in the mixed regime some agent pairs may play 0, 1, or 2 games per generation, an acceptable approximation of continuous-time dynamics.

05 · Evolution

Evolutionary Dynamics

Strategies don't mutate during a game, they evolve across generations. A simple imitate-the-best-neighbour rule produces surprisingly rich dynamics.

Selection Rule

After all games in a generation are played (and payoffs accumulated), each cell looks at itself and its 8 Moore neighbours. It adopts the strategy of whichever cell had the highest total payoff in that generation:

Strategy update rule $$s_i^{t+1} = s_j^{t} \quad \text{where} \quad j = \arg\max_{k \in \{i\} \cup \mathcal{N}(i)} \pi_k^t$$

This is a form of imitation dynamics, a deterministic selection rule equivalent to cultural evolution ("copy the most successful behaviour you observe"). It is closely related to the replicator equation studied in evolutionary game theory.

The Replicator Equation

In the mean-field (fully-mixed) limit, the continuous-time version of this dynamics converges to the well-known replicator equation. Let $x_s$ be the population share of strategy $s$. The rate of change is:

Replicator equation $$\dot{x}_s = x_s \left( f_s(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) \right)$$

Where $f_s(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{s'} x_{s'} \cdot \pi(s, s')$ is the fitness of strategy $s$ given the current population composition, and $\bar{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_s x_s f_s$ is the average fitness. Strategies above average grow; those below average shrink.

The spatial version replaces global averages with local neighbourhood averages, this makes clustering possible and dramatically changes the fixed points.

Mutation

After copying, there is a probability $\mu$ (the mutation rate) that the new strategy is replaced by a uniformly random strategy from the full set:

Mutation operator $$s_i^{t+1} = \begin{cases} \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{S}) & \text{with probability } \mu \\ s_j^{t} & \text{with probability } 1 - \mu \end{cases}$$

Mutation prevents strategies from going fully extinct and can restart evolutionary dynamics if the population reaches a stable (but suboptimal) mono-culture. Typical values: $\mu = 0.01$ (1% per generation).

Memory Reset on Strategy Change

When an agent's strategy changes (due to imitation or mutation), its interaction history is cleared. This is biologically motivated: a strategy change represents a fundamentally different behavioural program, and accumulated expectations about specific opponents (TFT's Grim Trigger flag, Pavlov's last-move memory) no longer apply. 

if self.agents[i].strategy != next_strategies[i] {
    self.agents[i].strategy = next_strategies[i];
    self.agents[i].history.clear();  // O(k) where k = num neighbours seen
    self.agents[i].age = 0;
}
self.agents[i].payoff = 0.0;       // reset every generation
Rust

Population-Level Metrics

The simulation tracks several aggregate statistics per generation:

Cooperation rate $$\rho_C = \frac{\text{number of C moves recorded in histories}}{\text{total moves recorded}}$$
Average payoff by strategy $$\bar{\pi}_s = \frac{1}{|I_s|} \sum_{i \in I_s} \pi_i$$

Where $I_s$ is the set of agents currently playing strategy $s$. These are computed each generation and plotted in the live strategy distribution chart.

Parallel Game Resolution

Game outcomes are read-only with respect to agent state, each game only reads the two agents' histories, not modifying them. This means all games can be computed in parallel: 

let results: Vec<_> = interactions.par_iter().map(|&(i, j)| {
    if i == j { return None; }

    let move_i = self.agents[i].decide_move(j, self.generation);
    let move_j = self.agents[j].decide_move(i, self.generation);
    let (p_i, p_j) = self.calculate_payoff(move_i, move_j);

    Some((i, j, move_i, move_j, p_i, p_j))
}).collect();
// Then apply payoffs single-threaded (sequential, no data races)
Rust

Rayon's par_iter() automatically splits the work across all CPU cores. The subsequent history/payoff update is done sequentially (no parallelism needed, the bottleneck is the game resolution, not the bookkeeping).

Parallel Reproduction

The reproduction phase (selecting next strategy per cell) is also embarrassingly parallel: each cell only reads its own payoff and its neighbours' payoffs (no writes during the pass). 

let next_strategies: Vec<Strategy> = (0..n).into_par_iter().map(|i| {
    let mut best = (i, self.agents[i].payoff);
    for &n in &self.get_neighbors(x, y) {
        if self.agents[n].payoff > best.1 { best = (n, self.agents[n].payoff); }
    }
    let mut strategy = self.agents[best.0].strategy;
    if rng.gen::<f32>() < self.config.mutation_rate {
        strategy = *strategies.choose(&mut rng).unwrap();
    }
    strategy
}).collect();
Rust

Reproducible Seeded RNG

The initial strategy placement uses a seeded StdRng, ensuring that the same seed always produces the same initial grid, critical for reproducible experiments: 

use rand::{SeedableRng, rngs::StdRng};

let mut rng = StdRng::seed_from_u64(config.seed);
// All initial strategy assignments use this seeded RNG
Rust

During simulation steps, each Rayon thread uses its own thread_rng() (a fast, non-deterministic RNG) to avoid the overhead of passing a mutable seed around across threads.

Rendering Pipeline

The grid is rendered as a batch of coloured rectangles submitted to egui's painter:

1. Cull off-screen cells

Only cells whose cell_rect intersects the viewport rect are added to the shape list, O(visible cells) rather than O(all cells).

2. Compute screen rect

$\text{screen\_pos} = \text{viewport.min} + \text{pan} + (x \cdot cw,\ y \cdot ch)$, where $cw = (W / \text{grid\_width}) \times \text{zoom}$.

3. Select colour

Strategy mode: each strategy has a fixed RGB colour. Heatmap mode: intensity $= \pi_i / \pi_{\max}$, mapped to a blue→red gradient.

4. Submit batch

ui.painter().with_clip_rect(rect).extend(shapes), a single draw call for all visible cells, using egui's retained-mode batching.

CSV Export

The Exporter struct opens the CSV file in append mode and writes a header row on the first call, then a data row (strategy counts) on each subsequent call. This allows incremental export during long runs without buffering the entire history in memory.

08 · Observations

What to Expect When Running

The simulation is rich enough to surprise you every run. Here are the patterns to look for, and the parameter combinations that reveal them most clearly.

Nowak & May Patterns (spatial cooperators)

With $N = 1.0$ (fully local), TFT and AllC agents form compact clusters called cooperative islands. These were first described by Nowak & May (1992) who showed that spatial structure alone, no punishment, no reputation, can sustain cooperation.

  • Set Neighbourhood N = 1.0
  • Default payoffs (T=5, R=3, P=1, S=0)
  • Watch TFT (blue) form tight clusters that resist red (AllD) invasion
  • Kaleidoscopic patterns emerge as clusters grow, collide, and fragment

AllD Dominance (mean field)

At $N = 0.0$, the system approximates the mean-field. AllD agents consistently invade and spread. This matches the classical prediction: in a well-mixed population with no repeated interactions, defection wins.

  • Set Neighbourhood N = 0.0
  • Watch AllD (red) quickly spread to dominate within 20–50 generations
  • Then observe the payoff collapse as the P-P equilibrium is reached

Grim Trigger's Brittleness

Grim Trigger (orange) is theoretically optimal, it sustains cooperation with minimal defection. But it is fragile under mutation: a single mutant defector starts a cascade of permanent punishment chains. Increase mutation rate to $\mu = 0.05$ to observe this.

Pavlov's Resilience

At intermediate locality ($N \approx 0.7$) with moderate mutation, Pavlov (purple) often outperforms TFT. Its Win-Stay, Lose-Shift rule allows it to recover from mutual defection spirals, unlike Grim Trigger which cannot forgive.

Inject Experiments

Use the right-click injection tool to draw a cluster of a specific strategy mid-simulation. Interesting experiments:

  • Start an AllD monoculture (reset, then paint the whole grid with AllD) → inject a small TFT cluster, can cooperation spread? (It can, at high N!)
  • In a stable TFT population, inject a large Grim Trigger region, does it take over?
  • Drop a Random blob into a cooperating population, how quickly does it equilibrate?
The deep insight: Cooperation is not a fragile accident. Given space, memory, and repeated interaction, exactly the conditions organisms live in, cooperation is a robust, attractor-state of evolutionary dynamics. This simulation lets you explore just how robust.
